# Weekend Capture Checklist — The Fly Trap

*Phone-friendly checklist for your visit. Open on your phone when you walk in.*

**Gear:** iPhone, native Camera app, 4:3 stills. Tap to focus; slide exposure down slightly to protect highlights in the red walls. For shots that matter: one straight-on, one slightly wider, one close-up detail.

**On permission:** You're a regular, they know you, they've eaten your cookies. You don't need to announce what you're doing, but a friendly "working on a design project, mind if I shoot a few things while we're here?" to whichever front-of-house person seats you keeps it warm. If Kara is on shift, this is a conversation opener, not a permission ask — see the pitch section at the bottom.

## P0 — Must-haves (don't leave without these)

### Artwork on the walls (the single most important asset)
- [ ] Every individual fly painting shot **straight-on, dead-center, filling the frame** (no angle skew)
- [ ] A **wider contextual shot** of each painting in place on the wall (shows scale + framing + wall color)
- [ ] Any **signatures or artist marks** on the pieces — close-up
- [ ] **Ask: "Who painted these?"** Get the artist name. If we can commission them for website spot illustrations, the site becomes visually inseparable from the room.
- [ ] Any **non-fly artwork** on the walls (other paintings, prints, framed press, photographs)
- [ ] **Overall wall shots** showing how the art is arranged as a whole

### The bar
- [ ] **Top-down close-up of the marble inlay** — fill the frame, get color variation
- [ ] **Straight-on of the full bar length** (wide establishing shot)
- [ ] **Seated bar-stool POV** — customer's-eye view
- [ ] **Bar stools** detail
- [ ] **Back-bar** (coffee machines, register, anything on the wall behind the bar)

### Seating areas
- [ ] **Wide shot of the main dining room** from the entrance (first impression)
- [ ] **Booth detail** — upholstery, table, place setting, window view
- [ ] **Table setting** close-up with salt-and-pepper shakers, menu, condiments in frame
- [ ] **A window table** with natural light (site hero candidate)
- [ ] **The overall flow** from entry → seating → back of house (depth shot)

### Salt-and-pepper shaker collection
- [ ] **Full shelf/display** in one shot
- [ ] **3–4 close-ups** of the weirdest individual shakers

### Checkerboard floor
- [ ] **Straight-down, filling the frame** (pattern detail for CSS reference)
- [ ] **Wide leading toward the back** (depth + rhythm)

### Red walls
- [ ] **Clean, unbroken section** with good lighting (needed for color calibration — the hex values in the design doc are placeholders until we sample from your photo)

### Yellow-green exterior
- [ ] **Straight-on from across Woodward** (wide storefront)
- [ ] **Signage close-up**
- [ ] **The door / entry**
- [ ] **Facade at golden hour** if time allows

### Your plates (everything you order)
- [ ] **Overhead with natural light**, before you eat
- [ ] **Secondary 45° angle** ("magazine shot")
- [ ] Order strategically: gingerbread waffles, The Forager, pho, any Swat Sauce on table — these are the site's hero dishes

### The printed menu (critical)
- [ ] **Full menu** — every page, flat on table, even natural light. This is the canonical source for the website Menu page.
- [ ] **Ask for a take-home copy** if available. If not, photograph every page twice (once straight-on for OCR, once at a slight angle to verify)
- [ ] **Specials board / chalkboard** if there is one
- [ ] **Any printed drink or dessert menus** (separate from main menu)
- [ ] **Kids' menu** if available

### The receipt (new — confirms the "Under Old Management" tagline)
- [ ] **Ask for an itemized receipt** when you pay, even if you're paying with card
- [ ] **Photograph the receipt in full** (every line, including header, footer, any tagline, any URL or social handle printed)
- [ ] **Close-up of the "Under Old Management" text** or equivalent — we want to see the exact typography, placement, and wording
- [ ] **Look for any other branded printed material** (napkins, coasters, takeout bags, stickers, promo cards, business cards at the register) and photograph those too
- [ ] **Note any tagline variants** — receipts, signage, and merch may not all use the same wording. If you see "Under Old Management" in three places with different typography, capture all three.

The receipts are a primary brand source right now. The website needs to echo whatever voice and typography they already use there.

## The visit, if Kara is on shift

You're not pitching this weekend. The plan is to build the entire site first and offer the finished product to Kara as a gift, with no commercial commitment until after she's seen it. That changes what you do if you run into her.

**What you're doing this weekend:**

- Gathering content: photos of the artwork, the room, the bar, the menu, the receipt
- Picking up intel: IG account authority, artist name, Toast confirmation
- Staying in relational good standing — the usual warm regular visit

**What you're NOT doing this weekend:**

- Showing or describing the design concept
- Committing to a deliverable timeline
- Making any commercial ask

**If Kara is on shift, the minimal flag:**

> "Hey Kara — I've been tinkering with a little project on my own time that I want to show you when it's ready. Nothing to share yet, just wanted you to know it's coming. Mind if I grab a few photos of the space while we eat?"

That's it. No specifics. No timeline. The word "tinkering" does a lot of work — it sets the register low so the eventual reveal exceeds expectations instead of meeting them. "My own time" removes any concern that you're invoicing hours against her without permission. "Mind if I grab a few photos" is the only ask.

If she asks what the project is, redirect gently:

> "Honestly, I'd rather show you than describe it. Give me a few weeks."

Curiosity is good. You want her curious when you come back with a finished site.

**If a front-of-house regular asks:**

You can be slightly more open since they're not the decision-maker:

> "Working on a side project — a little website redesign concept, just for fun. Don't tell Kara yet, it's a surprise."

This spreads positive anticipation without breaking the surprise. Some of them will tell Kara anyway; that's fine — "someone in the staff is secretly building us a website" is a good thing for her to hear.
